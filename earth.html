About Our Planet

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known astronomical object to harbor life. About 29% of Earth's surface is land consisting of continents and islands, while the remaining 71% is covered by water, mostly by oceans but also lakes, rivers, and other fresh water. Earth's atmosphere consists mostly of nitrogen and oxygen, which is vital for life as we know it.

Earth's outer layer is divided into several rigid tectonic plates that migrate across the surface over many millions of years. The planet's interior remains active with a solid iron inner core, a liquid outer core that generates Earth's magnetic field, and a convecting mantle that drives plate tectonics.

The Goldilocks Planet

Earth orbits in the Sun's habitable zone, at just the right distance to maintain liquid water on its surface. Several factors make Earth uniquely suited for life:

Atmospheric Composition
Magnetic Field Protection
Stable Climate System
Tectonic Activity
Liquid Water Abundance

Earth's large moon stabilizes the planet's axial tilt, preventing extreme climate variations. The magnetic field deflects harmful solar radiation, while the ozone layer blocks ultraviolet rays. These factors, combined with Earth's geological activity and water cycle, create the perfect conditions for life to flourish.

Earth's Biosphere

Earth's biosphere has significantly altered the planet's atmosphere and other abiotic conditions, enabling the proliferation of aerobic organisms as well as the formation of the ozone layer which blocks harmful ultraviolet light. The biosphere is divided into biomes, inhabited by broadly similar plants and animals.

Life on Earth first appeared as early as 4.1 billion years ago. Over 99% of all species that ever lived on Earth are extinct. Estimates of the number of species on Earth today vary widely; most species have not been described. More than 7.8 billion humans live on Earth and depend on its biosphere for survival.

Earth's Structure

Earth's interior, like that of the other terrestrial planets, is divided into layers by their chemical or physical properties:

Crust: 5-70 km thick
Mantle: ~2,900 km thick
Core: ~3,400 km radius

The outer layer is a chemically distinct silicate solid crust, which is underlain by a highly viscous solid mantle. The crust is separated from the mantle by the Mohorovičić discontinuity. The thickness of the crust varies: averaging 6 km under the oceans and 30-50 km on the continents.

Earth's Moon

Earth's only natural satellite is the Moon, which orbits Earth at 384,400 km (1.28 light seconds) and is roughly a quarter as wide as Earth. The Moon always faces Earth with the same side through tidal locking and causes the tides by gravitational attraction.

The Moon's gravitational influence stabilizes Earth's axial tilt, which has helped maintain a relatively stable climate over billions of years. The Moon was formed 4.51 billion years ago, shortly after Earth, by a giant impact between Earth and a Mars-sized body called Theia.

Interesting Facts About Earth

  • Earth is not a perfect sphere - it's an oblate spheroid, bulging slightly at the equator.
  • The highest point on Earth is Mount Everest (8,848 m) and the deepest is the Mariana Trench (10,994 m).
  • Earth's rotation is gradually slowing at about 17 milliseconds per hundred years.
  • There are more than 3,000 operational satellites currently orbiting Earth.
  • The Earth travels through space at 107,000 km/h (67,000 mph) in its orbit around the Sun.
  • About 300 million years ago, Earth had just one supercontinent called Pangaea.
  • The coldest temperature ever recorded was -89.2°C (-128.6°F) at Vostok Station, Antarctica.
  • Earth is the densest planet in the Solar System (5.51 g/cm³).
