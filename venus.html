About Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun and Earth's closest planetary neighbor. Named after the Roman goddess of love and beauty, Venus is the only planet named after a female figure. Despite being called Earth's twin due to their similar size and composition, Venus is a hellish world with extreme temperatures, crushing atmospheric pressure, and toxic clouds.

Venus rotates in the opposite direction to most planets (retrograde rotation) and has the slowest rotation period of any major planet. A day on Venus (one full rotation) actually lasts longer than a year on Venus (one orbit around the Sun). The planet's thick atmosphere traps heat in a runaway greenhouse effect, making it hotter than Mercury despite being nearly twice as far from the Sun.

Extreme Atmosphere

Venus has the densest atmosphere of the four terrestrial planets, consisting mostly of carbon dioxide with thick clouds of sulfuric acid. The atmospheric pressure at the surface is 92 times that of Earth's - equivalent to the pressure found 900 meters (3,000 feet) underwater on Earth.

The upper cloud layers of Venus move around the planet every four Earth days in what is called "super-rotation." These clouds reflect most sunlight, making Venus the brightest natural object in Earth's night sky after the Moon. Beneath the clouds, the surface is shrouded in perpetual gloom with visibility similar to a very overcast day on Earth.

Volcanic Landscape

Venus has more volcanoes than any other planet in the solar system, with over 1,600 major volcanoes or volcanic features. While most are dormant, there is evidence that some may still be active. The planet's surface is relatively young, estimated to be 300-500 million years old, suggesting a global resurfacing event occurred.

Venus features two major highland "continents" (Ishtar Terra and Aphrodite Terra), mountain ranges, vast lava plains, and numerous volcanic features including shield volcanoes, pancake domes, and "coronae" - circular structures formed by upwelling magma. The highest mountain, Maxwell Montes, rises 11 km (7 mi) above the average surface elevation.

Exploration of Venus

Venus was the first planet to be visited by a spacecraft (Mariner 2 in 1962) and the first to be successfully landed on (Venera 7 in 1970). Over 40 spacecraft have explored Venus, including NASA's Magellan which mapped 98% of the surface with radar in the early 1990s.

Upcoming VERITAS mission

Current missions include Japan's Akatsuki orbiter (since 2015) and upcoming missions like NASA's VERITAS and DAVINCI+, and ESA's EnVision, all scheduled for launch in the late 2020s to study Venus's atmosphere and surface in unprecedented detail.

Interesting Facts About Venus

  • Venus is the brightest planet in Earth's sky, often called the "Morning Star" or "Evening Star."
  • A Venusian day (243 Earth days) is longer than its year (225 Earth days).
  • If you could stand on Venus, the Sun would rise in the west and set in the east due to its retrograde rotation.
  • Venus has no moons or rings.
  • The surface temperature is hot enough to melt lead and zinc.
  • Venus may have had oceans in its early history that evaporated as the planet heated up.
  • Lightning has been detected in Venus's atmosphere, but it's unclear if it strikes the surface.
  • Some scientists speculate that microbial life might exist in Venus's upper cloud layers.
