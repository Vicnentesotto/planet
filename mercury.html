About Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and innermost planet in the Solar System. Its orbit around the Sun takes only 87.97 Earth days, the shortest of all the planets in the Solar System. It is named after the Roman deity Mercury, the messenger of the gods.

Mercury's surface resembles that of Earth's Moon, scarred by many impact craters from collisions with meteoroids and comets. Mercury has no natural satellites. The planet has virtually no atmosphere to retain heat, causing surface temperatures that vary more than on any other planet in the Solar System.

Physical Characteristics

Mercury is one of four terrestrial planets in the Solar System, meaning it is a rocky body like Earth. It is the smallest planet in the Solar System, with an equatorial radius of 2,439.7 kilometers (1,516.0 mi). Mercury is even smaller than some natural satellites, such as Jupiter's Ganymede and Saturn's Titan.

Mercury's core has a higher iron content than that of any other major planet in the Solar System, and several theories have been proposed to explain this. The most widely accepted theory is that Mercury originally had a metal-silicate ratio similar to common chondrite meteorites, but lost most of its silicate mantle early in the history of the Solar System through a giant impact.

Surface and Geology

Mercury's surface is dominated by impact craters, basaltic rock, and smooth plains. The largest known crater is Caloris Basin, with a diameter of 1,550 km (960 mi). The impact that created Caloris was so powerful that it caused lava eruptions on the other side of the planet and left a concentric ring over 2 km tall surrounding the impact crater.

Mercury's surface also features unusual geological formations called "hollows" - shallow, irregular depressions that appear bright and may be formed by the sublimation of volatile materials exposed by impacts. These features are unique to Mercury and not seen on other planetary bodies.

Extreme Temperature Variations

Mercury has the most extreme temperature variations in the Solar System because it has no significant atmosphere to retain heat. Daytime temperatures can reach 427°C (800°F), hot enough to melt lead, while nighttime temperatures drop to -173°C (-280°F).

Despite being the closest planet to the Sun, Mercury isn't the hottest planet (Venus holds that title) because it has almost no atmosphere to trap heat. Some polar craters near Mercury's poles remain permanently in shadow and may contain water ice, as detected by Earth-based radar and confirmed by NASA's MESSENGER spacecraft.

Exploration of Mercury

Mercury has been visited by two spacecraft: Mariner 10 in 1974-75 and MESSENGER (MErcury Surface, Space ENvironment, GEochemistry, and Ranging) between 2008-2015. The European Space Agency's BepiColombo mission, launched in 2018, is currently en route to Mercury and will arrive in 2025.

The MESSENGER mission revealed many surprises about Mercury, including evidence of water ice in permanently shadowed polar craters, volcanic activity that shaped much of its surface, and a magnetic field that is offset from the planet's center.

Interesting Facts About Mercury

  • Mercury has the most eccentric orbit of all planets, ranging from 46 to 70 million km from the Sun.
  • A year on Mercury (one orbit around the Sun) is just 88 Earth days, but a day (sunrise to sunrise) lasts 176 Earth days.
  • Mercury's iron core makes up about 85% of the planet's radius (compared to Earth's core being about 50%).
  • If you could stand on Mercury when it's closest to the Sun, the Sun would appear three times larger than it does from Earth.
  • Mercury has a very thin exosphere made up of atoms blasted off its surface by solar radiation and micrometeoroid impacts.
  • The planet has a weak magnetic field, about 1% as strong as Earth's.
  • Mercury's surface gravity is about 38% of Earth's - a 100 lb person would weigh only 38 lbs on Mercury.
